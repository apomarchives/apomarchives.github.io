How to Preserve Digital Evidence for Family Court (Before It Disappears)

By Kyērstin O’Neal  •  May 9, 2026  •  8 min read

← Back to Blog

Every week I hear the same story. Months of documentation -- threatening messages, harassment, coordinated digital abuse -- and an attorney who just said none of it is usable.

The evidence is real. The problem is how it was collected.

Digital evidence in family court is challenged, excluded, and dismissed constantly -- not because it’s fabricated, but because it wasn’t preserved correctly. Courts have standards. Those standards exist because screenshots are easy to fake, easy to alter, and contain no verifiable origin data. If your only documentation is a camera roll full of screenshots, you’re starting from a position opposing counsel will tear apart before the hearing starts.

Here’s what courts actually require, what kills evidence before it reaches a judge, and what you need to do right now.

Why Digital Evidence Gets Thrown Out

Three problems come up in nearly every case.

The first is metadata. Every digital file -- a photo, a message export, a screenshot -- contains metadata: timestamps, device information, location data, file modification history. When you screenshot something and save it, most of that metadata is stripped or overwritten. What’s left is a flat image with no verifiable origin. Opposing counsel knows this.

The second is chain of custody. Authentic evidence that can’t be proven unaltered is still vulnerable. Chain of custody documentation creates a paper trail -- who collected it, when, how, and who has handled it since. Without it, you have a standard objection waiting to be made.

The third is how the evidence was obtained. Accessing another party’s accounts -- even a spouse’s -- to gather evidence can constitute a federal crime under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, regardless of the relationship. Evidence collected this way isn’t just inadmissible. It creates criminal exposure for the person who collected it.

The Preservation Window Is Shorter Than You Think

Platforms have automated deletion policies and they don’t pause for your case. Facebook removes deleted messages from its servers within 90 days. Instagram Stories disappear from servers within 30 days. Snapchat deletes messages immediately after viewing. Most platforms purge account data within 30 to 90 days of deactivation. Cell carriers retain SMS records but require a subpoena to produce them.

When that window closes, the evidence is gone. A preservation letter sent before the deletion cycle runs triggers a legal hold -- but only if it goes to the right contact, references the correct legal authority, and gets there in time. A generic letter sent to the wrong address gets ignored. The clock doesn’t stop while you figure that out.

Preservation letters and subpoenas are not the same thing. A preservation letter asks the platform to hold the data. A subpoena compels production. You typically need both, in that order, and the preservation letter has to go first.

What Proper Preservation Actually Looks Like

Step 1: Document what exists before you touch anything

Write down every platform, account, and type of content relevant to the case -- specific posts, message threads, profile information, location check-ins, tagged photos. Date-stamp everything. This becomes your evidence inventory.

Step 2: Use platform-native export tools for your own accounts

Most major platforms offer a data download feature. These exports include metadata that screenshots do not -- timestamps, IP addresses, message delivery and read receipts. Export to a secure location and do not alter the files. These tools only work on your own account data.

Step 3: Use forensic capture for another party’s public content

For evidence on public-facing posts or profiles belonging to another party, use forensic web capture -- not a standard screenshot. Forensic capture tools create timestamped, metadata-rich records that are defensible in court. A screenshot is not.

Step 4: Send preservation letters within 24 to 48 hours

Send preservation letters within 24 to 48 hours of identifying evidence on a platform you don’t control. Each major platform has different legal contact procedures, different response timelines, and different data retention policies. Platform-specific templates built with the correct language get responses. Generic letters don’t.

Step 5: Establish chain of custody from the moment of collection

Every piece of digital evidence needs a log -- who collected it, when, what method was used, and every person who has handled it since. For digital files, this includes cryptographic hash values that prove the file hasn’t been modified since collection.

When to Call a Professional

The steps above cover what you can do yourself. There are situations where that isn’t enough.

You need professional documentation when the evidence involves coordinated behavior across multiple platforms; when the opposing party has technical sophistication and may be covering tracks; when you’re preparing a federal law enforcement referral; when the evidence needs to survive expert cross-examination; or when you’re dealing with spoofed accounts, impersonation, or network-level harassment.

A federal-grade investigation doesn’t just preserve evidence -- it builds the full picture. Subject identification, network mapping, pattern analysis, and a documentation package structured for the specific jurisdiction and proceeding. Everything documented under attorney supervision with chain of custody intact from collection to court.

If you’re not sure whether what you have is preserved correctly, it probably isn’t. The time to find out is before the hearing, not during it.
