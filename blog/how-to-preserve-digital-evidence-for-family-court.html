How to Preserve Digital Evidence for Family Court (Before It Disappears)

By Kyērstin O’Neal  •  May 9, 2026  •  8 min read

← Back to Blog

Every week, someone contacts APOM Research with the same problem: they have months of screenshots documenting harassment, threatening messages, or coordinated digital abuse -- and their attorney just told them none of it is usable.

Not because the evidence isn’t real. It is. But because it wasn’t preserved correctly.

Digital evidence in family court cases is routinely challenged, excluded, or dismissed -- not on its merits, but on the method of its collection. This guide explains what courts actually require, what kills digital evidence before it reaches a judge, and the specific steps you need to take right now.

The core problem: Screenshots are easy to fabricate, easy to alter, and contain no verifiable metadata. Courts know this. If your only documentation is screenshots saved to your camera roll, you’re starting from a very weak position.

Why Digital Evidence Gets Thrown Out

Family law attorneys encounter three recurring problems with digital evidence submitted by clients:

No metadata. Every digital file -- a photo, a message export, a screenshot -- contains metadata: timestamps, device information, location data, and file modification history. When you screenshot something and save it, most of that metadata is stripped or overwritten. What remains is a flat image with no verifiable origin.

No chain of custody. Even if the evidence is authentic, can you prove it hasn’t been altered between the moment it was captured and the moment it’s presented in court? Chain of custody documentation creates a paper trail showing who handled the evidence, when, and how -- the same standard law enforcement follows. Without it, opposing counsel has a standard objection.

Platform terms block direct extraction. Most people don’t realize that accessing someone else’s accounts -- even a spouse’s -- to gather evidence can constitute a federal computer crime under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, regardless of your relationship. Evidence gathered this way is not only inadmissible; it can expose you to criminal liability.

The Preservation Window Is Shorter Than You Think

Social media platforms, messaging apps, and cloud services all have automated deletion policies. Here’s what most people don’t know:

  • Facebook deletes deleted messages from its servers within 90 days
  • Instagram Stories disappear after 24 hours by default (and from servers within 30 days)
  • Snapchat messages are deleted from servers immediately after viewing
  • Most platforms begin purging account data within 30–90 days of account deletion or deactivation
  • Cell carriers retain SMS records for 18 months to 7 years depending on carrier -- but require a subpoena to access

Once that window closes, the evidence is gone. A preservation letter sent to a platform before the deletion window triggers a legal hold -- but only if it’s sent to the right contact, references the correct legal authority, and arrives before the automated deletion cycle runs.

Important: Preservation letters are not the same as subpoenas. A preservation letter asks the platform to hold data; a subpoena compels them to produce it. You typically need both, in that order, and the preservation letter needs to come first.

What Proper Digital Evidence Preservation Looks Like

Step 1: Document before you act

Before taking any preservation steps, record what exists and where. Write down every platform, account, and type of content relevant to your case: specific posts, message threads, profile information, location check-ins, tagged photos. Date-stamp your documentation. This becomes your evidence inventory.

Step 2: Use platform-native export tools

Most major platforms offer a data download or export feature. These exports include metadata that screenshots do not -- including timestamps, IP addresses, and message delivery/read receipts. Export to a secure location and do not alter the files.

  • Facebook/Instagram: Settings → Your Facebook Information → Download Your Information
  • Twitter/X: Settings → Your Account → Download an archive of your data
  • Google: takeout.google.com
  • iCloud: privacy.apple.com → Request a copy of your data

Note: these tools only give you access to your own account data. They cannot be used to capture evidence from another party’s accounts.

Step 3: Preserve, don’t just screenshot

For evidence that exists in someone else’s public-facing posts or profiles, use a forensic web capture tool rather than a standard screenshot. Tools like the Wayback Machine, Page Vault, or a professional investigator using specialized capture software create timestamped, metadata-rich records that are far more defensible in court.

Step 4: Send preservation letters immediately

If you believe relevant evidence exists on a platform that you don’t control, a preservation letter should go out within 24–48 hours of identifying that evidence. These letters are platform-specific -- each major platform has different legal contact procedures, different response timelines, and different data retention policies.

Attorneys who don’t have platform-specific templates on hand often send generic letters that are ignored or misrouted, costing critical time.

Step 5: Establish and maintain chain of custody

Every piece of digital evidence should be accompanied by a chain of custody log: who collected it, when, what method was used, and every person who has handled it since. For digital files, this includes cryptographic hash values (MD5 or SHA-256) that prove the file has not been modified since collection.

Key Takeaways
  • Screenshots alone are insufficient for court -- metadata and chain of custody are required
  • Platform deletion windows are 24 hours to 90 days depending on platform and content type
  • Preservation letters must be platform-specific and sent before the deletion window closes
  • Accessing another party’s accounts to gather evidence is a federal crime regardless of relationship
  • Platform exports capture metadata that screenshots do not -- use them for your own accounts
  • Hash values prove file integrity and are required for law enforcement submissions

When to Bring in a Professional

The steps above cover the basics of self-preservation. But there are situations where professional investigation is the right call -- and waiting too long is the most common mistake.

You need professional documentation when: the evidence involves coordinated behavior across multiple accounts or platforms; the opposing party has technical sophistication and may be covering their tracks; you’re preparing for federal law enforcement referral; the evidence needs to withstand expert witness cross-examination; or you’re dealing with spoofed accounts, impersonation, or network-level harassment.

A professional OSINT investigation doesn’t just preserve evidence -- it builds the full picture: subject identification, network mapping, pattern analysis, and a documentation package structured for the specific jurisdiction and legal proceeding you’re preparing for.
